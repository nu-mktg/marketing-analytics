Survival Analysis Explorer — Lecture 4

Adjust the hazard rate λ and the Weibull shape parameter k to see how survival curves and hazard functions change. When k = 1 the model reduces to the exponential (constant hazard). k > 1 means increasing hazard over time (burn-in / aging); k < 1 means decreasing hazard (early attrition). Relevant to Homework 4: churn prediction.

Exponential (k = 1): S(t) = e−λt, h(t) = λ (constant)
Weibull: S(t) = exp(−(λt)k), h(t) = kλ(λt)k−1
Median survival: t½ = (ln 2)1/k / λ

Distribution Explorer (Poisson & Gamma) — Lecture 8 (BG/NBD)

The BG/NBD model assumes each customer's purchases follow a Poisson process with rate λ, and that λ varies across customers according to a Gamma distribution. Explore how the distribution shapes change with the parameters. Relevant to Homework 8: Customer Lifetime Value modeling.

Poisson PMF: P(X = k) = e−λ · λk / k!    (purchase counts per period)
Gamma PDF: f(x; r, α) = αr · xr−1 · e−αx / Γ(r)    (α = rate parameter; heterogeneity in λ)
Gamma mean = r/α    Gamma variance = r/α²    (BG/NBD default: r=0.85, α=8.2 → mean rate ≈ 0.10/period)

Fourier Seasonal Decomposition — Lecture 10 (Prophet)

Prophet models seasonality as a sum of sine and cosine waves (Fourier series). Increase the number of harmonics to see how additional terms create more detailed seasonal patterns. The thick line is the total signal (trend + seasonality); coloured thin lines show individual harmonic components. Relevant to Homework 10: Demand forecasting with Prophet.

y(t) = trend(t) + Σn=1..N [ an cos(2πnt/P) + bn sin(2πnt/P) ] + ε
P = seasonal period (52 weeks), N = number of Fourier harmonics
More harmonics → finer detail; fewer harmonics → smoother seasonal curve
